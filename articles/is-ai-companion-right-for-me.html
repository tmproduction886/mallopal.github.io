Is an AI companion right for me?

A lot of people ask this before trying one. The short answer: it depends on what you want. If you're looking for a low-pressure daily check-in, something that remembers what you share, and a small ritual that fits around real life—especially if you live alone, work remotely, or are in a new city—then an AI companion might be a good fit. It's not a replacement for friends, family, or professional support. Below we answer the question in plain language, then point you to a free 5-question quiz so you can see for yourself.

Who are AI companions good for?

They tend to work best for people who want:

  • A small, steady ritual. A 2–5 minute check-in that doesn't demand more than you have to give.
  • Something that remembers. Not a fresh chat every time—a thread that builds over days and weeks so it feels continuous.
  • Proactive check-ins. You don't always have to start the conversation; the companion reaches out so the relationship feels present.

That often matches people who live alone, work from home, have just moved cities, or simply want one low-pressure place to be noticed without performing. If that sounds like you, read more in our guide on loneliness, remote work, and AI companions.

Who might not need one?

An AI companion is not a substitute for therapy, crisis support, or real relationships. If you're in acute distress or need professional mental health care, please reach out to a qualified provider or crisis line first. And if you already have plenty of daily connection and don't feel the need for another thread to maintain, you might not need one—and that's fine.

How do I know if I should try one?

Ask yourself: When do I feel the most alone during my week? and Would a gentle, short check-in actually feel supportive?
